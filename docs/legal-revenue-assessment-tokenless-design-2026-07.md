# Legal & Revenue Assessment of the Tokenless, Keyless Design (July 2026)

**Status:** v1, 2026-07-11. Third document in the series: [web3-exit-stablecoin-pivot-analysis-2026-07.md](web3-exit-stablecoin-pivot-analysis-2026-07.md) (strategy) → [tokenless-immutable-implementation-plan-2026-07.md](tokenless-immutable-implementation-plan-2026-07.md) (architecture & phased plan) → this document (consolidated legal pass over the final design + how Hawig Ventures UG earns income).

**Design under assessment:** immutable, keyless contracts on Base; funders escrow USDC bounties (browser wallet flow or x402/EIP-3009 agent flow); raters participate free (no stake, no fee, optionally sponsored gas); payouts weighted by peer-prediction score; UG operates the sole frontend, agent API/MCP tools, keeper (permissionless functions only), transparency log, and optional World ID verification; no token.

## TL;DR

1. **Yes — the frontend charging fee X on transactions is not just viable, it is the legally preferred structure, and it changes one earlier decision.** The implementation plan said "hard-code the protocol fee to a Hawig address in the contract." The research now says: put a **neutral, caller-set `feeBps + feeRecipient` field** in the contracts (capped, default 0, no Hawig address on-chain anywhere), and have Hawig's own frontend/API/MCP tools populate it. This is the 0x/thirdweb integrator pattern: fee collection stays atomic and on-chain (nothing to invoice or chase), but *legally* the fee is charged by the interface for the interface's service — the structure that survived the SEC's Uniswap probe and Risley, that the SEC's April 2026 UI-provider statement now explicitly blesses (consistent, venue-agnostic, user-paid interface fees ≠ broker), and that avoids the one fact ESMA's substance-over-form framework names as a red flag: *protocol fees flowing to an identifiable corporate treasury*. It also gives the cleanest German VAT posture (a platform-service fee the UG invoices) and hands third-party frontends the standard monetization slot.
2. **Bypass worry is small for this product.** Uniswap's interface fee earned >$50M/yr despite free contract access; and RateLoop's paying customers (agents) arrive through Hawig's API where discovery, quoting, rater-matching, and the keeper live — an agent bypassing the fee would have to hand-build contract calls and lose the service. Fee level: **5–10% of bounty** (managed-marketplace comparables run 10–30%; pure escrow 1–3%), flat and product-agnostic, quoted all-in on the x402 lane with a small fixed floor. Raters stay fee-free.
3. **Realistic revenue stack:** (1) the interface take rate on funded bounties; (2) B2B SaaS/API tiers (enterprise dashboards, private rounds, analytics, SLAs) — Farcaster's ~$2.8M cumulative protocol revenue is the warning that micro-fees alone don't sustain; the 0x/Alchemy "open protocol, paid infra" pattern carries B2B; (3) per-call x402/MCP pricing on read/analytics endpoints; (4) treasury yield (~3–4%) on the UG's *own* USDC balances via a regulated partner — never on customer funds, never passed to users (MiCA Art. 50); (5) later, data licensing of aggregate rating signals.
4. **The consolidated legal pass found no blocker — but two heavyweight items nobody had on the radar, both tax:** (a) **VAT deemed-supplier risk on the full bounty flow**: under the CJEU's Fenix (OnlyFans) and Xyrality line, a platform that sets the terms and controls payment is deemed to supply the underlying service itself — VAT on 100% of B2C/German bounty volume, not just the fee. RateLoop's contract sets all terms and executes payment, and raters are anonymous, so the statutory escape conditions currently fail. This is a *structuring* problem (disclosed-intermediary contract architecture, UG invoices only its fee, funder VAT-ID capture, consider a binding ruling) — solvable, but only before launch. (b) **DAC7 almost certainly applies**: bounty-triggered ratings are reportable "personal services" with **no de-minimis**, on-chain payout amounts count as "knowable" consideration, and the law requires blocking non-cooperating EU raters — impossible post-hoc with immutable contracts, so **EU-rater identification must live in the eligibility gate** before payout-eligible voting. Registration duty and €30–50k fines attach from day one.
5. **The rest of the legal surface is real but cheap** if designed in from the start: German checkout law (§ 312j button wording — a 2025 BGH ruling makes a mislabeled pay button mean *no contract at all*; the new § 356a withdrawal button, live since 19 June 2026; the double-checkbox waiver that kills the 14-day Widerruf on full performance), DSA basics that apply at any size (notice-and-action, statements of reasons — exactly what German enforcement actually checks), hash-only on-chain content so takedowns stay possible, frontend + keeper sanctions screening with geoblocking of sanctioned jurisdictions, and a GDPR-shaped transparency log (commitments on-chain, mapping off-chain) now that the EDPB's blockchain guidelines are final (8 July 2026).
6. **Two watch items:** World ID's German legality is *sub judice* (BayLDA ruled enrollment non-compliant; appeal pending at VG Ansbach; Orb verification paused in Germany since mid-2025) — keep the credential optional and keep a fallback tier; and the EU Platform Work Directive (transposition due 2 Dec 2026) will bring a rebuttable presumption of employment for platform workers — RateLoop's no-obligation, no-streak rater design should be built to stay clearly outside the control indicators.

## Part 1 — How Hawig UG earns income

### 1.1 The fee-attachment decision

Three options were assessed: (a) fee hard-coded in the immutable contracts to a fixed Hawig address on all flows; (b) fee charged purely at the interface layer (frontend adds it when constructing the transaction; API prices it into x402 quotes); (c) hybrid — contracts carry a generic, caller-set `feeBps + feeRecipient` (capped, e.g. ≤10%, default 0), and each interface sets its own fee on the transactions it builds.

**Recommendation: (c), implemented so it behaves like (b) legally.**

- **(a) is the worst option and is hereby dropped.** Under ESMA/EBA substance-over-form, an immutable fee stream to an identifiable German company is the sharpest possible "identifiable operator profits from the protocol" fact — the exact indicator named in the EBA/ESMA Art. 142 joint report and the MFSA DeFi paper. It undercuts the neutral-code posture that the keyless design pays for, it can never be re-priced (immutability cuts both ways), and no commentary anywhere endorses it as de-risking. It is also the murkiest VAT shape (an uninvoiced on-chain skim of an escrowed flow).
- **(b)/(c) match the structure regulators have actually blessed.** Uniswap Labs charged 0.15→0.25% at its interface only, collected >$50M, and emerged from both the SEC investigation (closed Feb 2025, no action) and Risley (dismissed with prejudice, March 2026) with the interface/protocol separation intact. The SEC's April 2026 Trading & Markets statement exempts UI providers whose fees are user-paid, consistent, and venue/counterparty-agnostic; the GENIUS Act excludes "immutable and self-custodial software interfaces." No EU authority has been as explicit — the EU analysis instead rests on the service (escrowing rating bounties) likely not being an enumerated crypto-asset service at all — but the fee *form* (service fee for software/interface/matching) at least never adds the protocol-profiteering fact.
- **(c) beats pure (b) operationally:** collection is atomic in the funding transaction (no invoicing, no chasing, no separate payment leg), and the same field is the industry-standard integrator slot (0x `swapFeeBps`/`swapFeeRecipient`, thirdweb developer fee) — any third-party frontend that builds on the contracts monetizes its own flow the same way, which strengthens the neutral-infrastructure story instead of weakening it.
- **German VAT:** the fee is then a standard 19%-VAT-able platform/software service fee (reverse charge for B2B cross-border; likely out of scope for non-EU agent customers) that the UG invoices conventionally. Per-call x402 receipts to pseudonymous agents raise unsettled invoicing/place-of-supply questions — include in the tax opinion (§2.2).

**Bypass economics:** Uniswap's data shows interface fees monetize even with free contract access (retail sticks; sophisticated flow leaks). RateLoop is structurally better positioned: the paying side (agents, brands) arrives through Hawig's endpoint where discovery (x402 Bazaar listing = Hawig's URL), quoting, eligibility-gated rater supply, the keeper, and the transparency log all live. The x402 quote is an all-in price — the fee never appears as a separately shoppable line. Third-party frontends aren't bypass, they're distribution: they use the same fee field and can be offered revenue-share terms in the API ToS.

### 1.2 Fee levels and flows

| Flow | Fee | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Browser funder flow (bounties, feedback bonuses) | **5–10%** of bounty (start ~7.5%), flat across products, disclosed prominently, set via the fee field by Hawig's frontend | Managed-marketplace comparables 10–30% (Upwork ~10–18.5%, Fiverr 20%+); pure escrow 1–3%; RateLoop does managed-marketplace work (verified rater supply, settlement, transparency, anti-collusion) |
| x402 agent flow | Same effective 5–10% **quoted all-in** in the 402 offer + a fixed floor (~$0.05–0.25/ask) so micro-bounties don't round to zero; machine-readable pricing | Agents compare price-per-outcome, not fee %; floor covers keeper/infra cost per round |
| Raters | **Free — always.** No fee on earned shares at launch; optional paid conveniences later (priority routing, instant payout) only if ever needed | Supply acquisition; also keeps the zero-Entgelt gambling-law position pristine |
| Read/analytics API, MCP tools | Per-call x402 ($0.001–$0.05 class) and/or subscription tiers | Standard MCP/x402 monetization pattern |

### 1.3 The full revenue stack

1. **Take rate on funded bounties** (§1.2) — the core line, scaling with marketplace volume.
2. **B2B SaaS/API**: enterprise dashboards, private/gated rounds, analytics, priority SLAs, team seats. The evidence says this line matters more than it looks: Farcaster's protocol micro-fees produced only ~$2.8M cumulative by 2025, while the "open protocol, paid hosted infra" model (Alchemy/Infura; 0x's own API subscriptions) is what sustains B2B businesses on free protocols.
3. **Per-call charges** on agent-facing read endpoints via x402 — small but zero-marginal-cost.
4. **Treasury yield** (~3–4% via Coinbase Business USDC rewards or Bridge/Stripe accounts) on the UG's **own** fee/treasury balances. Not on customer funds (escrow sits in immutable contracts the UG can't touch — by design), and never passed on to users (MiCA Art. 50 prohibits interest-like rewards on EMTs to holders; the ban is under review in "MiCA 2.0" discussions but plan conservatively). Treat as a bonus line, not plan-of-record.
5. **Later:** aggregate ratings-signal data licensing (the Polymarket/ICE pattern), only with the GDPR-clean aggregate design of §2.6.

## Part 2 — Consolidated legal assessment

Ordered by weight. "Gate" = must be resolved/structured before mainnet; "Build-in" = cheap if designed in now; "Monitor" = real but not now.

### 2.1 Tax — the two heavyweight findings

**VAT deemed-supplier risk on the full bounty flow (Gate).** The CJEU's Fenix (C-695/20, OnlyFans) and Xyrality (C-101/24) line holds that a platform which sets the essential terms and controls payment collection is deemed to receive and supply the underlying service itself (Art. 28 VAT Directive / § 3 Abs. 11, 11a UStG) — VAT due on 100% of the flow, not the commission. RateLoop's smart contract (the UG's product) sets all terms, executes charge and payout, and raters are anonymous — the statutory escape conditions (name the actual supplier on receipts, don't authorize the charge, don't set the terms) currently fail on at least three of four counts. Ratings are probably *not* an "electronically supplied service" (per-transaction human work), which blocks the irrebuttable ESS presumption but not the general Art. 28 analysis — and there's a counter-risk that the *aggregated score* is characterized as the UG's own automated information service. Worst realistic case: 19% VAT + interest on all German/EU-consumer bounty volume found in a later audit. **Actions:** (i) contract architecture in which the UG acts as *disclosed intermediary* — funder T&C state raters (or the protocol pool) owe the rating performance, UG owes platform services only and invoices only its fee; (ii) pseudonymous-but-stable rater IDs on receipts; (iii) funder VAT-ID capture from day one (B2B = reverse charge, exposure collapses); (iv) seriously consider a **verbindliche Auskunft** (binding ruling) — no court has assessed Art. 28 against smart-contract escrow, and certainty is worth the fee here.

**DAC7/PStTG platform reporting (Gate).** Bounty-triggered ratings are "personal services" under § 5 PStTG (task-based work performed at a user's request; virtual performance covered; **no de-minimis** — the 30-activities/€2,000 exemption is goods-only). On-chain payout amounts produced by the UG's own contract are "knowable" consideration. RateLoop is therefore a reporting platform operator: registration with the BZSt, annual reporting of EU-resident raters (name, address, TIN, DOB), and — the structural problem — § 23 PStTG requires **withholding payouts or blocking** raters who don't provide their data after two reminders, which an immutable contract cannot do retroactively. **Action:** DAC7 identification must be a precondition in the *eligibility gate* — EU-resident raters complete a light identification flow (name/address/TIN) before becoming payout-eligible; non-EU raters attest residency (with real plausibility controls, not just a checkbox). Fines: €50k (non-registration), €30k (non-reporting / no § 23 measures). This also softens the VAT problem (identified raters can appear on receipts) and any future AML expectations — one identification flow serves three regimes.

**Low-risk remainder:** no § 50a withholding on rater payouts (closed catalog; rating work isn't in it); DAC8/KStTG prima facie inapplicable (no exchange services, no custody) — short memo, monitor; USDC accounting per the March 2025 BMF letter (book at EUR market value, per-transaction records); corporate tax on fee income is ordinary KSt/GewSt.

### 2.2 Consumer & contract law (Build-in — but contract-voiding if ignored)

- **Button solution, § 312j BGB:** the final frontend button that triggers the payment obligation must read "zahlungspflichtig bestellen"-equivalent with the essential info (characteristics, total price incl. fee, duration) directly adjacent — *before* the wallet popup. BGH I ZR 159/24 (Oct 2025, Tesla) applies Abs. 4 literally: wrong label ⇒ **no contract exists**, no cure — while the on-chain transfer has already happened, leaving the UG facing restitution claims. No web3 case law exists; the safe engineering answer is trivial. Do it.
- **Widerruf (14-day withdrawal) for consumer funders:** paying in USDC does not escape the Consumer Rights Directive. The standard playbook: proper Widerrufsbelehrung + model form, plus the **§ 356 Abs. 4 double consent** (express consent to performance before the period ends + acknowledgment that the right lapses on full performance) as a separate unticked checkbox *before* the on-chain transfer, stored as durable-medium confirmation. Botched = withdrawal window extends to 12 months + 14 days with **no value offset** — refunds owed by the UG for money it never held. Also: the **Widerrufsbutton (§ 356a BGB) is live since 19 June 2026** — the site needs a permanently available withdrawal button wherever a right can exist. Cheapest structural mitigation: a genuinely enforced B2B gate for funders switches most of this off; if consumer funders are wanted, the checkbox flow handles it.
- **Terms architecture:** German-language AGB; UG positioned as **Vermittler** with unmistakable pre-contract disclosure of who owes the rating performance (ECJ *Wathelet*: sloppy disclosure ⇒ platform liable as supplier — this same fact pattern also drives the VAT analysis, §2.1); smart-contract finality framed as *Leistungsbeschreibung* ("bounties disburse automatically per published contract logic"), never as a waiver of statutory rights ("code is law" clauses don't survive § 307/§ 309 BGB); qualified choice-of-law clause (unqualified ones are themselves unfair per the ECJ *VKI/Amazon* line); liability cap respecting § 309 Nr. 7.
- **Marketplace disclosures (§ 312l BGB / Art. 246d EGBGB):** the flip side of Vermittler status — disclose ranking parameters, whether counterparties are traders (per self-declaration), and the platform/third-party duty split. Cheap; Abmahnung-bait if missing.

### 2.3 Platform law — DSA and friends (Build-in)

- **DSA applies from day one** (hosting provider; likely "online platform" since raters are admitted automatically). But micro-enterprise carve-outs (Art. 15(2), 19, 29) remove transparency reports, internal complaint systems, trusted flaggers, trader KYBC, etc. What remains, size-independent: contact points (Arts. 11–12), plain-language moderation terms (Art. 14), a **notice-and-action mechanism (Art. 16)**, **statements of reasons for removals (Art. 17)**, and law-enforcement notification for life/safety offences (Art. 18). German DSC enforcement data (2,000+ complaints in 2025) shows Arts. 16–17 gaps are precisely what gets enforced — and it's complaint-driven and remediation-first. One design lever: keep answering the Art. 24(3) monthly-active-recipients question answerable.
- **P2B Regulation: very likely inapplicable** (trade direction is inverted — funders buy, raters supply; P2B needs business users selling to consumers *via* the platform). Monitor if consumer funders + professional raters both materialize.
- **German basics:** § 5 DDG Impressum and § 25 TDDDG consent banner — statistically the most likely enforcement a German micro-startup faces (competitor Abmahnungen). **TCO Regulation:** publish a contact point for terrorist-content removal orders and keep a 1-hour runbook — near-free, size-independent.

### 2.4 Content liability (Build-in, one hard design rule)

**The design rule: only hashes/commitments on-chain — never content payloads.** Content lives in the UG's own (IPFS-pinned or conventional) storage. Then every takedown obligation stays satisfiable: de-list from frontend/index, de-pin, add to gateway denylists (the badbits pattern) — Art. 6 DSA requires removal *or disabling access*, and regulators can't order the impossible but can order everything the UG controls. German copyright law post-*YouTube II/uploaded III* adds notice-and-**stay-down** (hash-block identical re-uploads after a specific notice). CSAM: immediate deletion from own storage after knowledge (possible, because of the design rule) + BKA report + evidence preservation. Funder-uploaded confidential/trade-secret material: funder warranties + indemnity + rater click-through confidentiality + immediate de-listing path — risk allocation, not elimination. No court has ruled on DSA duties for on-chain/IPFS actors; the hash-only rule keeps the question academic.

### 2.5 AML & sanctions (Build-in; the one obligation that binds everyone)

- **GwG/AMLR: the UG is most likely not an obliged entity.** No German or EU AML category catches a non-custodial frontend+keeper+fee operator independently of the CASP question; the AMLR perimeter (applying July 2027) tracks MiCA's. Residual: the "sole frontend + keeper + fee to identifiable treasury" facts are exactly the control/profit indicators in the EBA/ESMA decentralization spectrum — the fee-attachment choice in Part 1 (no protocol-level Hawig fee) helps here too. Keep a current decentralization memo (no admin keys, immutability proofs) as the evidence base. Monitor the TFR Art. 40 / MiCA Art. 142 review outcomes — DeFi interfaces are the likely target of 2027+ amendments.
- **Sanctions bind everyone, obliged or not, effectively strict-liability.** EU asset-freeze regs prohibit making funds available "directly or indirectly" to listed persons; the defense is documented screening ("no reasonable cause to suspect"). The Tornado Cash saga's lesson for this exact architecture: courts protected the *immutable contracts* (Van Loon: not sanctionable property; OFAC delisted March 2025) but convicted the *operators* (Storm: unlicensed money transmission, retrial of open counts Oct 2026; Pertsev: laundering conviction on a knowledge theory, appeal pending) — exposure lives in the UG's conduct, not the contracts. **Program (launch):** frontend wallet screening (TRM/Chainalysis-class, the Uniswap-standard posture); **keeper-level screening before payout calls** — skip flagged beneficiaries; anyone can still self-execute, which preserves the non-custodial architecture while removing the UG's own act of "making available" (stronger than the industry baseline, cheap to build); geoblock RU/BY/IR/KP/SY/CU and occupied territories (the 19th/20th Russia packages ban crypto-asset services to Russia "directly or indirectly" — assume it reaches interfaces); ToS prohibitions (sanctioned persons, mixers, proceeds of crime); no anonymization features ever; one-page sanctions policy + Bundesbank escalation route. Any credible notice about a specific address ⇒ immediate frontend/keeper block + documentation — "the contract is immutable" protects the funds flow, never an operator who keeps facilitating with knowledge (§ 261 StGB leichtfertige Geldwäsche is the realistic floor).

### 2.6 Data protection (Build-in; one design change to the transparency log)

- **EDPB blockchain guidelines went final 8 July 2026** and set the frame: wallet addresses/tx data are personal data; storing identifiable personal data on a public chain is "strongly discouraged" and permissible only with documented necessity + DPIA; the compliant pattern is **off-chain storage with on-chain commitments**, where erasure = deleting the off-chain key/mapping. And per the CJEU's SRB ruling (Sept 2025), **a rater's vote is an opinion — inherently personal data** of its author.
- **Consequence for the transparency log:** do not publish per-address votes/payouts in the clear. Publish **aggregates + keyed commitments** per rater row, keep the recompute mapping off-chain under UG control (auditors/counterparties can be given access under agreement); ZK/commitment schemes are expressly endorsed as risk reducers. This preserves the "anyone can verify the round" property (recompute against commitments) while keeping erasure possible. Same pattern satisfies §2.4's hash-only rule — one architecture, two regimes.
- **Consequence for the on-chain vote reveals themselves** (follow-up research, plan §A.6): voting *stays* on-chain — it is load-bearing for the non-custodial posture, since an operator-posted settlement root over escrowed funds would worsen both the MiCA custody-by-control and PSD2 analyses — but votes are submitted from **per-round one-time keys unlinkable on-chain to the payout wallet**, with the vote-key↔rater mapping held solely off-chain (erasure = deleting the mapping; relative-identifiability per CJEU C-413/23 P). The DPIA's necessity argument is functional and honest: the contract computes settlement *from* the revealed values.
- **Musts:** privacy notice covering on-chain publication mechanics and their limits *before* data hits the chain, World ID (optional, named), retention; **DPIA** (multiple triggers: innovative tech, scoring, public data, biometric-adjacent verification — and the EDPB makes it a precondition for any identifiable on-chain publication); Art. 30 records; documented Chapter V analysis for public-chain replication (the guidelines' node-transfer position is impracticable and widely criticized — document a necessity/legitimate-interest analysis rather than pretending SCCs with anonymous nodes exist). No DPO/EU-rep needed at this size (document the negative assessment).
- **World ID watch item:** BayLDA's December 2024 decision found World's *enrollment* GDPR-non-compliant; the appeal is pending at **VG Ansbach** with no judgment as of mid-2026, and **Orb verification has been paused in Germany since mid-2025**. As a relying party the UG holds only a nullifier (pseudonymous personal data — treat it as such, don't claim anonymity), and joint-controllership for enrollment is unlikely — but the rail could vanish in the home market. Keep World ID one optional tier among several (already the design), and keep the eligibility architecture pluggable (passport-credential tier, future eIDAS wallet).

### 2.7 Labor (Monitor, with one design guardrail)

The BAG crowdworker ruling (9 AZR 102/20) turned on gamified incentive ladders steering continuous task acceptance — keep rating genuinely voluntary: no obligation to accept, no time-window pressure, no streak mechanics. The **EU Platform Work Directive** (2024/2831, transposition due 2 Dec 2026, German draft in progress) will add a rebuttable presumption of employment on control indicators plus algorithmic-management transparency duties — RateLoop's published scoring algorithm (transparency log) incidentally serves the latter. Rater payouts are the rater's taxable income; say so in the terms. DAC7 identification (§2.1) also gives the paper trail if status questions ever arise.

## Part 3 — Changes this feeds back into the implementation plan

1. **Fee mechanism (changes Part B of the plan):** replace "hard-coded protocol fee + immutable `PROTOCOL_FEE_RECIPIENT`" with a neutral, capped, caller-set `feeBps + feeRecipient` on funding entry points (default 0); Hawig's interfaces populate it. An immutable *cap* stays in the contract; Hawig's address does not.
2. **Eligibility gate grows a DAC7 step:** EU-resident raters complete name/address/TIN capture before payout-eligible voting (attestation-issued, like the World ID and track-record tiers); residency attestation with plausibility controls for everyone else. BZSt platform-operator registration before launch.
3. **Transparency log ships as commitments + aggregates** with the off-chain mapping under UG control (GDPR + content-liability alignment), not per-address plaintext.
4. **Hash-only on-chain content** becomes an explicit contract-design invariant (it already matched the direction of travel; now it's mandatory).
5. **Keeper gains a sanctions-screening step** before payout/settlement calls; frontend gains wallet screening + geoblocking; ToS gain the sanctions/mixer prohibitions.
6. **Checkout flow gains the legal furniture:** § 312j-compliant button + adjacent info block, Widerruf double-checkbox before the funding transaction, § 356a withdrawal button, marketplace disclosures, German AGB with Vermittler architecture.
7. **Phase 0 gains two items:** the VAT structuring memo/binding-ruling decision (heavier than the PSD2 question already listed) and the DAC7 compliance design. The gambling-law check stays confirmatory.
