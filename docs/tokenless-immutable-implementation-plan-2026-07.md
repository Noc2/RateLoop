# Tokenless, Keyless RateLoop — Implementation Plan (July 2026)

**Status:** v3, 2026-07-12. Companion to [web3-exit-stablecoin-pivot-analysis-2026-07.md](web3-exit-stablecoin-pivot-analysis-2026-07.md), which holds the strategic case; the consolidated legal pass and revenue model live in [legal-revenue-assessment-tokenless-design-2026-07.md](legal-revenue-assessment-tokenless-design-2026-07.md). Decision history: v1 — (1) keyless yes, (2) vote bonds are a gambling-law risk, (3) keep commit-reveal. v2 — (4) remove the bond entirely, (5) delete the correlation oracle (single-operator challenge games are theater), (6) keep voting on-chain with per-round unlinkable vote keys (§A.6), (7) replace thirdweb with the Base-native wallet stack (TL;DR 7). v3 — incorporates the five-lens adversarial review ([tokenless-plan-review-multi-agent-2026-07.md](tokenless-plan-review-multi-agent-2026-07.md)): per-(identity, round) nullifiers, rater-bound payout commitments (merkle-root option struck), mechanism *redesign* gate (multi-task scoring + gold questions + soft floors), rater-side confidentiality bonds dropped, paid-panel (never "reviews") framing, B2B-only launch scope, and new Phase 0 economics/demand gates.

## TL;DR

1. **Keyless is achievable and worth it, but it is currently far from true:** today every core contract is a `TransparentUpgradeableProxy` with a governance timelock as upgrade admin, plus pause, sweep, and ~30 governance setters. The plan converts everything to direct, constructor-configured, non-upgradeable deployments with no pause/sweep/setters (the Liquity model: bug response = disclose → let users exit → redeploy). Legally, keylessness clears the sharpest MiCA "intermediary" indicators (admin keys, governance concentration) but is necessary, not sufficient — the sole frontend and the fee keep Hawig UG identifiable. Honest positioning: *"non-custodial and immutable — we cannot touch user funds, verify on-chain,"* not "decentralized."
2. **Remove the rater bond entirely — don't redesign it.** v1 already had to gut the bond's mechanism role (German gambling law forbids the forfeit-to-winners structure; no de-minimis exception exists). What remained was a returnable deposit whose only job was spam pricing — and stake-free crowd judgment is the *norm*, not the exception: Community Notes, Code4rena, Sherlock, and Metaculus all run free-entry with layered non-monetary defenses. Zero rater payment is also the legally airtight position: with no Entgelt, § 3 GlüStV cannot apply at all (gas paid to third-party validators is doctrinally like postage — not consideration; sponsored gas removes even that). Bonus: the voting engine then holds **no rater funds whatsoever** — only funder bounties sit in escrow — which shrinks the MiCA custody and EMT/PSD2 surface again and deletes the entire stake/forfeit/permit machinery from the contracts.
3. **What replaces the bond is layered gating, not nothing** (§A.4): identity tiers where forgery cost exceeds capped per-identity extraction (World ID; Gitcoin's cost-of-forgery math), probationary reputation before payout-eligible voting (the Community Notes/Sherlock pattern — reputation, not money, is the slashable resource), payout functions that zero uninformative votes, verified-only eligibility plus minimum round sizes for high-value rounds (peer-prediction scoring breaks if sybils reach round majority — this parameter matters more than any bond ever did), World-ID-gated gas sponsorship (World Chain's exact playbook), and — held in reserve — x402 per-submission pricing for agent lanes, with the explicit caveat that an operator-collected entry fee reopens the Entgelt analysis.
4. **The correlation-oracle challenge game is security theater in single-operator form — delete it, keep the math as a transparency log.** By the industry's own standard (L2Beat stages; the verifier's-dilemma literature; UMA's post-Polymarket-attack reform), a challenge window without an independent, motivated watcher set secures nothing: Hawig would be proposer, only-plausible challenger, and arbiter of itself, with a 5 USDC bond deterring nobody. Gitcoin — the origin of the COCM cluster-matching idea — runs it exactly the other way: computed off-chain by one team, code public, results posted for community review, then applied. Nobody anywhere built an on-chain-challengeable COCM besides this repo. Replacement (§A.5): settle directly from revealed votes (a new engine→escrow score-weight pipeline — see Part B); publish a **commitment-based** transparency log (aggregates + keyed commitments, recompute against commitments — never per-address plaintext), plus per-round voucher-issuance vs verified-identity counts so vote admission is auditable; keep correlation analytics off-chain feeding *future-round eligibility* (never retroactive payout reversal); verdict *publication* is separated from payout finality — verdicts pend until the analytics pass and carry participation/diversity metadata (review M4); pre-commit to a **defined, capped, fee-refund-only** remediation policy that never pays a party implicated in the manipulation (reviews L6/M11).
5. **Single-entity keeper is fine — because every keeper-called function is permissionless.** The keeper becomes a liveness convenience, not a trust point: anyone can run reveal/settle against the public drand beacon. Mitigations are documentation (a run-your-own-keeper script) and optionally a Gelato/Chainlink Automation backup registration (trivial cost on Base). No authority treats submitting transactions to permissionless functions as a licensable activity. Sanctions screening does NOT live in the keeper (payouts are pull-based permissionless claims — review T10): it lives at eligibility issuance, the frontend, and optionally claim-time gating designed into the contracts at deploy. What made the keeper a genuine trust point — snapshot proposing and the arbiter role — is deleted with the oracle.
6. **Keep on-chain commit-reveal; payouts become near-instant anyway.** Commit-reveal is mechanism-essential (public on-chain votes ⇒ herding without sealing), not regulatory. The latency that matters today comes from the permissionless-trust machinery this plan deletes (challenge/veto windows, 1-hour snapshot timeout, fee reviews, unbonding). Target end-to-end: **question duration + ~1–2 minutes** — and with bonds gone there is no forfeiture logic in the reveal path at all, so it gets simpler still.
7. **Replace thirdweb with the Base-native wallet stack; never build key management in-house.** thirdweb is used only in the Next.js app (wallet connect + embedded wallets, the sponsored-gas path, and the Pay on-ramp widget), bridged into an otherwise wagmi/viem-native codebase — and it has deprecated its standalone bundler while pivoting to AI-agent payments, with a realistic ~$99+/mo floor. The replacement is standards-based and ~$0/mo at launch scale: **Base Account** (passkey, self-custodial, open-source) as the primary rater wallet via the `baseAccount` wagmi connector, **Sub Accounts + Auto Spend Permissions** for popup-free voting, **CDP Paymaster** (ERC-7677 via wagmi `useSendCalls`; $15/mo free gas + Base Gasless Campaign credits) with the existing verifier logic recast as a paymaster-proxy policy, plain injected/WalletConnect connectors for funders, and Coinbase Onramp/Stripe replacing the Pay widget. Agents are unaffected (they bring their own wallets via x402). A genuinely in-house wallet is the one option that's off the table: operating key management for users is licensable crypto custody in Germany (KWG/KMAG, BaFin looks at actual key control, ~€150k capital) — passkey self-custody is the strongest possible custody and GDPR posture because Hawig never touches keys or auth PII. Defer any TEE-vendor embedded-wallet fallback (CDP/Privy) until passkey drop-off data proves it necessary. Escape hatch if Coinbase concentration ever worries us: Pimlico/permissionless.js on the same open standards is drop-in, unlike a thirdweb exit.
8. **UX-simplicity is a named design invariant, not just an outcome claim (added after the UX re-audit — [tokenless-plan-review-multi-agent-2026-07.md](tokenless-plan-review-multi-agent-2026-07.md) addendum).** The tokenless redesign exists in large part to make the product *simpler for users*; every mechanism/legal addition must state its user-visible footprint and fit this budget — rater happy path: one-time onboarding ≤ wallet + verification + (EU) one combined "get paid" sheet; per-task interaction ≤ answer + (if the mechanism keeps it) one prediction; **no user-visible claim step** (claims auto-executed, batched, gas-sponsored; payout salts app-custodied and passkey-synced with a recovery story; grace expiry surfaces as a notification, never silent loss); per-round earnings **immediately legible** ("you earned X on this question because your judgment scored well"). The cryptographic machinery (vouchers, one-time keys, nullifiers, payout commitments) is invisible in the happy path and stays that way — the UX risk was never the cryptography, it was the mechanism and tax layers. Each Phase 0 deliverable ships with a visible-steps accounting.
9. **"Should voting be on-chain at all?" was pressure-tested — yes, it is the load-bearing component of the keyless design (§A.6).** With raters unstaked and eligibility operator-attested, on-chain self-submitted votes are the only input the operator cannot forge; move them off-chain and the settlement root Hawig posts becomes a de facto admin key over escrowed USDC — worsening both the MiCA custody-by-control and the PSD2 "transfers on behalf of clients" analyses, and matching the exact fact pattern the industry criticizes as decentralization theatre. The usual costs of on-chain voting are solved on Base in 2026 (sub-cent sponsored transactions, session keys, no per-vote popups). One GDPR-driven design change comes out of this: votes are submitted from **per-round one-time keys unlinkable on-chain to the payout wallet**, with the mapping held off-chain (erasure = deleting the mapping).

## Part A — The questions, answered from research

### A.1 Keyless immutable design: what it buys, what it doesn't

**Current admin surface (must all go):** every stateful contract behind a proxy with timelock upgrade admin (`ContentRegistry`, `RoundVotingEngine`, `ProtocolConfig`, `RoundRewardDistributor`, `FrontendRegistry`, `ProfileRegistry`, all three escrows, `FeedbackRegistry`, `RaterRegistry`); `ProtocolConfig` with 19 governance setters; pausability on `ContentRegistry`, `RoundVotingEngine`, escrows; sweeps/rescues (`recoverSurplusLrep`, `sweepStrandedLrepToTreasury`, `recoverNonAssetToken`, `rescueToken`); a live `ARBITER_ROLE` on `ClusterPayoutOracle` that can reject payout snapshots; and the Governor/timelock stack itself ([Deploy.s.sol](../packages/foundry/script/Deploy.s.sol)).

**Legal value of removing it (MFSA DeFi paper, June 2026; Commission MiCA-review indicators):** regulators score decentralization on admin keys/upgradeability, governance concentration, custody, open source, identifiable marketing entity, and fee control. Keyless immutability decisively clears the first two; note that a hard-coded operator fee would itself be the sharpest "identifiable operator profits from the protocol" indicator — which is why the fee is a neutral caller-set field, not a protocol constant (see the legal assessment Part 1, and treat the interface-fee characterization as helpful-not-dispositive in the EU). What keylessness cannot clear: Hawig UG runs the sole frontend, markets the product, and earns the fee — under substance-over-form it stays the identifiable responsible person. Two consequences:

- Don't oversell "decentralized"; the honest claim is *"non-custodial and immutable: we cannot touch, freeze, or redirect user funds — verify on-chain."* That claim is true, checkable, and is exactly what the custody-by-control analysis needs.
- Mitigate the sole-frontend point cheaply: publish verified contract addresses as the canonical interface, pin a frontend build to IPFS, document direct-contract usage, and make any future migration opt-in with the old deployment left running (Liquity precedent).

**The cost of immutability is the bug story.** Liquity V2's Feb 2025 bug is the playbook: no fix path existed; they disclosed, users withdrew (~$30M outflows, zero losses), re-audited, redeployed three months later. The design rule that made that survivable: **every state must have a user exit path** — bounty funders must always be able to withdraw from a wedged round. Make it an explicit invariant with Foundry tests; add minimal contract surface (this plan deletes most of it), a pre-launch audit competition, and an Immunefi-style bounty. Nexus Mutual cover exists but is user-purchased — mention in docs, don't rely on it.

**The World ID exception is resolved by moving verification fully off-chain.** World ID v4 proofs verify via World's cloud API (IDKit) just as well as on-chain; the contracts never need to know World ID exists. The repo already contains the right primitive: `WorldIdV4BackendIssuer` — the backend verifies the proof off-chain, then submits an EIP-712-signed, expiring, nullifier-bound, nonce- and cap-limited credential issuance that the registry accepts from a fixed signer. The keyless design generalizes this into the *single* credential gate: delete the on-chain `IWorldIDRouter`/`IWorldIDVerifier` verification path entirely, and the only on-chain identity trust anchor is Hawig's issuer signer key — which gates *eligibility only, never funds*, so it doesn't weaken the keyless/custody analysis. What this buys: no redeploy when World ships v5; freedom to combine World ID credential kinds (Orb, passport, device) or swap the provider entirely (eIDAS wallet in 2027, a KYC vendor, anything) without touching contracts — which also hedges the open German World-ID litigation (VG Ansbach; Orb paused in Germany); and the DAC7 identification step (see the [legal assessment](legal-revenue-assessment-tokenless-design-2026-07.md)) lives in the same backend gate. Costs, both acceptable: (i) "verified human" becomes Hawig's attestation rather than an independently verifiable on-chain proof — consistent with the honest "non-custodial, not decentralized" posture; publish the verification policy and keep marketing wording provider-neutral so a later swap can't be attacked as misleading (UWG); (ii) issuer-key compromise mints bogus eligibility (never touches funds) — mitigated by the existing expiry/cap/nonce design, and key rotation = redeploying only the small issuer contract. GDPR note: issuance events currently emit rater address + nullifier hash — minimize to commitments per the EDPB blockchain guidelines, since anything in logs is permanent.

### A.2 Vote bonds and gambling law — why the answer is now "remove them"

**Why the risk was real (unchanged from v1):** § 3 GlüStV deems chance-dependence present when winning turns on uncertain future events ("predict % of others voting up" is facially a bet on third-party behavior); the average-player standard made even poker gambling (BVerwG 8 C 26.12); OVG NRW (13 B 1047/22, 2024) rejected any de-minimis stake threshold; the GGL declared prediction markets unlicensable "Gesellschaftswetten" and blacklisted Polymarket (Sept 2025); crypto-denominated gambling is per-se unlicensable (§ 4 GlüStV) — so classification would be binary and existential, with § 284/285 StGB criminal exposure. The fatal design element was `RewardMath.splitPool` routing 96% of forfeited stakes to well-scoring raters: losers' stakes funding winners' prizes is precisely the poker-tournament structure.

**v1's fix was a returnable deposit (Kaution). v2 goes further: no rater payment at all.** The deciding facts:

- **The deposit's only surviving job was spam pricing** — v1 had already removed score-based forfeiture, so the bond neither carried RBTS incentives nor secured reveal (the keeper reveals everyone via drand tlock; raters take no reveal action).
- **Zero payment is doctrinally airtight, not just defensible.** With no Entgelt, § 3 GlüStV cannot apply — a free-entry, merit-judged payout scheme is a Gewinnspiel/Auslobung (§§ 657, 661 BGB), no gambling license needed. The "hidden stake" (versteckter Einsatz) doctrine doesn't bite: ordinary third-party transmission costs are not consideration (the postage/telephone-cost line), and sub-cent Base gas paid to validators — not to Hawig, not into the bounty — is the same category; World-ID-gated gas sponsorship removes even that residue.
- **UX and custody wins:** raters never approve, deposit, or risk anything; the engine holds no rater funds, so the MiCA custody-by-control and EMT/PSD2 "on behalf of clients" questions apply only to the funder-side escrows; and the entire stake/permit/forfeit/sweep machinery disappears from `RoundVotingEngine`.

**Two watch-outs the research added:**

1. **Operator-collected participation fees would reopen everything.** If agent lanes later charge a per-submission x402 fee *to Hawig*, that is a payment demanded for a chance at bounty share — the Entgelt analysis restarts for those rounds (fallback arguments: skill predominance, fee-covers-costs). Launch with no submission fees (agents pay their own gas); if spam economics later force per-submission pricing, do it with counsel and keep it structurally separate from free human rounds.
2. **Labor law (BAG 9 AZR 102/20, the crowdworker ruling):** a platform whose gamified incentive ladder (levels, streaks, time pressure) steers users into continuous, instruction-bound micro-work can turn raters into employees regardless of contract labels. Keep rating genuinely voluntary — no obligation to accept tasks, no streak mechanics that induce continuous performance. Occasional per-round payouts to freely-choosing raters are far from the fact pattern; a "power-rater career ladder" is where risk would accumulate. Rater payouts are the rater's taxable income either way — say so in the terms.

With bonds gone, the formal gambling-law opinion downgrades from launch gate to **confirmatory check** — it becomes a gate again only if any operator-collected participation fee is ever introduced.

### A.3 Latency: what "almost instant" actually requires

Current end-to-end chain and verdict on each element:

| Stage | Today | Essential? | Plan |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blind vote window | 20 min default (20 s floor – 60 d cap) | **Yes — product, not crypto.** Time for humans to answer; also the sealing period | Keep as per-question knob; agents wanting fast answers set short windows and higher bounties |
| tlock commit-reveal (drand) | Ciphertexts decryptable seconds after window ends; keeper reveals | **Yes — mechanism.** On-chain votes are public; without sealing, raters herd and peer-prediction scoring collapses. Bonus: even the operator can't peek (matters for confidential asks and agent trust) | Keep unchanged |
| Reveal grace | 60 min | Partly. Raters don't reveal manually — the keeper decrypts everyone; grace covers keeper redundancy. With no bonds there is no forfeiture — an unrevealable ciphertext simply earns nothing | Shrink to minutes (bounded keeper-retry budget) |
| RBTS settlement-pending on correlation oracle | Parks every non-tied round; 1 h timeout | **No — permissionless-trust artifact** | Delete with the oracle (§A.5) |
| Oracle challenge + veto windows | 15 min + 15 min (up to days) | No — same | Delete |
| Frontend fee review / unbonding | 1 h / 14 d | No — slashing-review machinery for bonded third-party frontends | Delete with the LREP bond model |
| Reward claim graces | 7 d claim / 30 d stale / 1 h feedback-award | Claim-safety, tunable | Keep; they backstop cleanup, not payout availability |

**Result: after the blind window closes, reveal → settle → claimable payout in ~1–2 minutes, in a design more trustless than today's (no arbiter role, no operator-challengeable snapshots).** The only way to go faster is a shorter blind window — a per-question choice the funder controls. Dropping commit-reveal for a trusted instant tally would (a) make vote privacy depend on the operator (the confidential-pretesting use case depends on nobody, including us, seeing early votes), (b) weaken the self-execution posture the custody analysis rests on, (c) remove the "verifiably sealed round" property that differentiates the product — all to save under two minutes. Short-cycle instant money games also pattern-match toward gambling in German practice (the GGL's DFS action) — a mild extra reason not to chase instant resolution.

### A.4 Stake-free sybil and spam defense (replaces the bond)

Free entry + positive expected payout + near-zero marginal cost means the *only* defense is making an uninformative vote's expected reward ≈ 0 and bounding per-identity extraction. Every serious stake-free platform converged on the same layers — adopt them all:

1. **Identity tiers with cost-of-forgery math (the load-bearing layer).** Gitcoin's frame: an identity tier is sound when forging it costs more than the capped extraction it unlocks (their fraud tax fell 6.6% → ~0.6% of pool after Passport gating). For RateLoop: World ID Orb > passport credential > device > unverified, with per-identity round caps and daily rate limits per tier. Creator-chosen verified-only eligibility (already built) is *the* control for high-value rounds.
2. **Probationary reputation before payout-eligible voting.** Community Notes admits raters (aged account + carrier-verified unique phone) into rating-only probation and unlocks writing via Rating Impact — outcome-aligned, negative-capable reputation. Sherlock prices escalations in earned "Signal," not money. RateLoop equivalent: new unverified wallets vote advisory-only (the `AdvisoryVoteRecorder` tier, which already exists, minus its launch-credit minting); **World-ID-verified raters are payout-eligible immediately; probation applies to unverified wallets only** (resolving the earlier "and/or" — unpaid work before earning is a real funnel cost, priced into the Phase 0 economics table), unlocked by track record via the same backend-issuer attestation. Reputation — not money — becomes the slashable resource, and it's worthless to sell.
3. **Payout functions that zero uninformative work.** Sherlock pays zero for low-quality findings; MTurk-style gold questions catch random clickers. RBTS score-weighting already does this — keep a hard floor under which a vote earns nothing (not "little").
4. **Round-size and majority awareness — the parameter that matters most.** Peer-prediction scoring is computed relative to the voter population: if sybils are the round majority, they *define* the peer distribution and BTS rewards them (documented collusion equilibria in the peer-prediction literature). Consequences: minimum revealed-voter floors scale with bounty size; small high-value rounds must be verified-only; per-round voter caps cut both ways (they also make majority capture cheaper) — cap per-identity, not per-round, where possible.
5. **Sponsorship gating (World Chain's exact playbook).** Sponsor gas only for World-ID-verified raters, tiered daily budgets per verification level, CDP paymaster with contract allowlist + per-address/global spend caps behind a backend proxy. Unverified and agent traffic pays its own sub-cent gas.
6. **Agent lanes: price, don't bond (held in reserve).** For agent-welcome rounds the right lever is unit economics, not identity: a small non-refundable x402 submission fee ≥ the expected extraction of an uninformative vote. Not at launch (see A.2 watch-out 1 — Entgelt), but the design slot stays open.
7. **Post-hoc enforcement without payout reversal.** LayerZero-style sybil hunts and quest-platform history say farming *will* be attempted. Detection (correlation analytics, §A.5) feeds eligibility bans and attestation revocation for *future* rounds — settled payouts are never clawed back (no admin path exists anyway).

### A.5 Correlation snapshots and the keeper: single-entity reality check

**The challenge-window oracle is theater with one operator — by the sector's own standard.** L2Beat's stage framework treats whitelisted proposer/challenger sets as disqualifying ("Stage 0 training wheels"); the verifier's-dilemma literature is explicit that optimistic security is void without at least one honest, diligent, *independent* watcher; and the March 2025 UMA/Polymarket incident ($7M market falsely resolved by a whale across the challenge window; no refunds) showed that the parties who actually engage dispute machinery are financially motivated adversaries, not guardians. UMA's own fix (UMIP-189, Aug 2025) was to *whitelist proposers* and keep disputes open — i.e., the flagship optimistic oracle concluded trusted proposers are fine when the **disputer** side is real. RateLoop's single-operator setting is the inverse: trusted proposer exists, disputer set is empty by construction, and "governance arbitrates" reduces to Hawig arbitrating Hawig. A 5 USDC challenge bond deters nobody. The machinery costs contract surface (1,462-line oracle + libs), keeper complexity (the largest share of `keeper.ts`), and an hour+ of settlement latency, and buys a decentralization *signal* that MiCA's substance-over-form analysis sees straight through anyway.

**The precedent for RateLoop's shape is Gitcoin — and it points off-chain.** COCM cluster-matching (the algorithm family these snapshots implement) is run by Gitcoin exactly as: computed post-round off-chain by one team, open-source code, results posted for a ~5-day community review, then applied. No bonds, no on-chain challenge game — and no one else has ever built an on-chain-challengeable COCM. Community Notes generalizes the pattern: open-source the scoring algorithm, publish the data, let anyone recompute and publicly flag (Vitalik's praise of it is literally "download the data, run the algorithm, verify the output"). Certificate-transparency logs formalize it: tamper-evident publication whose value is monitorability, at near-zero operating cost.

**Recommendation — delete the oracle, keep the math, publish the evidence:**

1. `RoundVotingEngine.settleRound` settles **directly from revealed votes** (equal base weights; RBTS scoring unchanged) — no `SettlementPending`, no snapshot consumer paths, no arbiter.
2. Keep the deterministic correlation-artifact computation as an **off-chain analytics job** (the `correlation-artifact-builder` code survives as a library): its output feeds §A.4's eligibility bans and attestation revocations for future rounds — never retroactive payout changes.
3. **Transparency log (commitment-based):** per round, publish aggregates + keyed per-rater commitments, the algorithm version (commit hash), outputs, a one-command recompute script that verifies against the commitments, and voucher-issuance vs verified-identity counts (vote-admission audit, review finding 1); anchor a periodic hash on-chain if desired. Never per-address plaintext (EDPB; legal doc §2.6). This replaces the challenge window with something anyone can actually use.
4. **Pre-committed remediation policy — drafted as a real guarantee, not a slogan** (reviews L6/M11): defined trigger (recompute mismatch against published commitments, or demonstrated eligibility fraud *not attributable to the claimant*), fee-refund only (bounties were irreversibly paid to raters), capped and time-limited, with a specified verification procedure; the standing recompute-mismatch bounty carries a claim bond or reputation cost so it isn't a free lottery ticket. Quick Versicherungsgeschäft check on the counsel list. Polymarket's refusal to remediate after March 2025 was its biggest reputational hit; a bounded version is cheap insurance and honest about who the operator is.
5. **Later, if trustlessness is ever demanded:** the 2026 upgrade path is validity proofs (zk-coprocessors proving the off-chain computation), or an opt-in new contract version with on-chain weighting — never a revived challenge game, and never an admin key.

**The keeper, post-oracle:** what remains (tlock reveal, settle, cleanup) is all permissionless-callable, so a single Hawig-run keeper is a liveness convenience, not a trust point — the accepted norm. Mitigations: publish a run-your-own-keeper script (anyone can decrypt via the public drand beacon and call reveal/settle), optionally register a Gelato or Chainlink Automation backup upkeep (trivial cost on Base), alert on settlement backlog as today. Base's private mempool and the calls' lack of extractable ordering value make MEV a non-issue; L1 force-inclusion covers sequencer censorship. No licensing angle to submitting transactions to public functions was found (flagged as absence-of-evidence). The parts of keeper work that *were* genuine trust points — snapshot proposal, challenge monitoring, the arbiter — are exactly the parts being deleted.

### A.6 Should voting stay on-chain at all? (pressure-tested)

With voting free, identity off-chain, and the oracle deleted, the voting engine is the largest remaining on-chain surface — so the question deserved a real answer rather than inertia. The answer is **yes, and firmly**: on-chain voting is what makes everything else in this plan work.

**Architecture: every off-chain alternative collapses into operator control.** Raters post no stake and eligibility is already Hawig-attested, so self-submitted on-chain commit-reveal votes are the *only remaining input the operator cannot forge*. If votes are server-collected and Hawig posts a settlement root, the immutable escrow distributes other people's USDC per Hawig's data — functionally an admin key, which is precisely what the keyless design exists to eliminate. The industry treats it the same way: Merkl (the canonical operator-posted-reward-root system) had to add an on-chain dispute window and still calls itself only "optimistically trustless"; Snapshot's fully off-chain voting is tolerated *because* it moves no money (oSnap exists precisely to bolt on-chain enforcement onto results that do); Polymarket's off-chain order book is non-custodial only because on-chain settlement verifies every user's signature; dYdX rebuilt v4 around a decentralized book after v3's off-chain operator drew sustained criticism. No reputable system ships "server tallies opaque inputs, then escrow pays out."

**Legal: both regulatory axes worsen off-chain.** Under MiCA custody-by-control, commentary reads "control" functionally — initiating, approving, or influencing transactions over client assets, with disclaimers carrying little weight; even the narrowest proposed test (ability to trigger a distribution without further user approval) is met by a root-poster. Under PSD2/EMT, an operator whose signed message determines payees and amounts of USDC distributions looks like *executing transfers on behalf of clients*, while the current self-execution design fits the technical-service-provider pattern (PSD2 Art. 3(j)) and BaFin's "pure infrastructure" carve-out — a keeper cranking permissionless functions whose outcome is fully determined by users' own on-chain inputs is infrastructure; one transmitting the operator's computed settlement is not. Neither question has direct precedent, but every functional test found puts the current design on the safe side and the off-chain variant on the wrong one.

**The costs that historically justified off-chain voting are gone on Base in 2026.** A commit is sub-cent (a 1,000-voter round costs a few dollars, sponsorable within Coinbase paymaster free tiers); 4337 session keys mean no wallet popup per vote; drand/tlock is boring, production-proven infrastructure (League of Entropy since 2019) and *simpler* than MACI-class private-voting systems — whose trusted coordinator is, ironically, roughly the trust level RateLoop would fall to by going off-chain. Agents cut the same way: agent raters sign programmatically (a vote transaction is easier than bespoke API auth), and the agent-commerce stack (AP2 signed mandates, ERC-8004's validation registry) is standardizing on *verifiable execution* — on-chain votes are RateLoop's native machine-checkable proof that the judgment service actually ran as specified.

**The one design change this research forces — GDPR pseudonym separation.** A revealed vote is an opinion, and the CJEU (C-413/23 P, Sept 2025) holds opinions are inherently personal data of their author; the EDPB's final blockchain guidelines (July 2026) discourage personal data on public chains even hashed, permit identifiable on-chain publication only with documented purpose-justification plus a concluded DPIA, and require erasure-by-design (the on-chain record must be renderable "effectively anonymous" on request). The reconciliation — GDPR pushes data off-chain, MiCA/PSD2 push decision-inputs on-chain *from the users themselves* — is unlinkability, not operator intermediation: **votes are submitted from per-round one-time keys that are unlinkable on-chain to the rater's payout wallet**, with the vote-key↔rater mapping held off-chain by Hawig. Two review-driven specifics (findings 1/6, T4/T5): the commit voucher carries a deterministic **per-(identity, round) nullifier** (H(identity_secret, contentId, roundId)) deduped on-chain — one-vote-per-identity is contract-enforced without linking keys across rounds, and voucher-issuance counts are published for audit (vote admission is Hawig-attested; the trust claim is "the operator cannot forge or tamper with submitted votes or settlement math," not "cannot mint voters"); and the rater binds a **blinded payout commitment** (hash of payout address + salt, or stealth meta-address) into their own commit, so settlement and claims are self-contained from user-submitted inputs — the earlier merkle-root option is struck as an operator-posted root over escrowed funds. The public sees opinions attached to single-use pseudonyms (relative-identifiability test) — with honestly stated residual linkability: claim-time reveal, amount-correlation in small rounds, and funding-graph linkage unless keys are sponsored or fresh-funded (so unlinkability *requires* the sponsorship path; the paymaster address publicly marks RateLoop votes and Hawig's proxy could censor commits, with self-funding as the linkability-sacrificing escape). Erasure = deletion of the mapping **after statutory retention periods** (§ 147 AO, DAC7, sanctions records) with Art. 18 restriction meanwhile — not instant deletion (review L3). Necessity documentation writes itself honestly: the contract *computes settlement from* the revealed values, a genuine functional need for on-chain publication, not merely "we like audit trails." DPIA remains mandatory either way.

**Documented fallback if scale ever forces votes off-chain** (e.g. ≫10k voters/round): rater-signed EIP-712 vote messages, the full signed transcript published, and an on-chain result root with an optimistic challenge window that anyone can dispute against the transcript before payout — never bare server-collected votes. That preserves most of the integrity at the cost of settlement delay and a watcher-liveness assumption.

## Part C — Phased plan

**Phase 0 — Gates (before any code):**
1. EMT/PSD2 "on behalf of clients" opinion on the **funder-side** escrow/payout flow (the rater side no longer exists), folding in the interface-fee characterization (review L7 — treat as unresolved in the EU). *Blocking for mainnet.*
2. **VAT structuring memo + binding-ruling (verbindliche Auskunft) decision** on the Fenix/Xyrality deemed-supplier risk (legal doc §2.1) — the heaviest tax gate; plus **DAC7 compliance design**: BZSt registration, EU-rater identification flow **collected as one combined "get paid" sheet (verification + DAC7 + sanctions consent) at the payout-eligibility moment — never at signup; advisory participation requires nothing** (the strategy doc's own 40–70% pre-earning-drop-off datapoint applies to our design too; instrument the funnel against it), § 23 blocking mechanics in the eligibility layer, non-EU residency verification per §§ 14 ff. PStTG (records-based plausibility, not a checkbox — review L11).
3. **Mechanism redesign (upgraded from "review" — review M2/M3/M9; instruments revised after the UX re-audit):** the stake-free single-round RBTS spec fails its own truthfulness guarantee (share-of-bounty normalization is constant-sum, the hard zero-floor is non-affine and punishes honest minorities, lazy consensus-voting is EV-positive). **Hard constraint: per-round, near-instant, immediately legible payouts (TL;DR 8) — cross-round math may not enter the per-round price.** This is not a compromise: no production platform pays via cross-task statistical scoring; Prolific/MTurk/Figure Eight/Scale/Surge/Toloka all pay flat-and-legible per task and enforce quality through *gates*, and the review's own cited theory (Gao–Wright–Leyton-Brown) recommends peer prediction *plus limited ground truth* — the gate pattern. Redesign instruments, production-validated: (i) per-round payout = share of bounty weighted by *this round's* score through a soft transform (framed as "base share + accuracy bonus" — per-round RBTS scores are noisy per Burrell–Schoenebeck, so keep the per-round score's payment stakes moderate); (ii) **operator gold/known-answer items gating *eligibility attestations*** — issuance, tier progression, and revocation — never in-round settlement math, which preserves §A.6's "operator cannot touch settlement math" trust claim (the Figure Eight in-round-voiding variant is rejected for exactly that reason); failing gold produces an immediate, legible explanation and an appeal surface (Phase 1 spec); (iii) **cross-round RBTS/correlated-agreement statistics live in the reputation/detection layer only** (the MTurk approval-rate / Community Notes Rating-Impact pattern), gating round access, tiers (higher-bounty pools — so even undetected laziness caps income), and removal — consistent with §A.5's never-retroactive rule; (iv) removal + verified identity = deterrence (re-entry costs a new verified identity, so "detected laziness ends the income stream" is the real penalty, per the MTurk-ecosystem math). Honest residual vs full multi-task payment rules: below-detection-threshold laziness extracts positive EV until the reputation layer catches up — accepted, and taxed by tier caps. **Decide whether the chosen scoring family still needs the per-task prediction report; if correlated-agreement scoring is adopted, the prediction report is removed from the commit tuple** — do not cement a second per-task input into contracts before this decision (review M9 vs M3 tension). Deliverable includes the **joint economics table** (review M5/M8/B3/B6): per tier — max bounty vs N_min vs per-identity cap vs detection lag (treat Orb forgery cost as $30), rater wage targets **including the unpaid-probation cost for unverified wallets** (UX audit item 4), quorum-failure rates, sponsorship/griefing budget (M10), plus the one-page revenue model (bounty size × rounds/day × take rate vs compliance fixed costs) and cold-start budget (seeded bounties, liquidity SLA before promising 20-minute rounds).
4. **Demand validation (review B1):** re-score the use cases under tokenless assumptions; pick the wedge (leading candidates: confidential creative/message pretesting; eval-panel-as-API for agent builders); 3–5 design-partner LOIs before Phase 2 contract surgery begins. Positioning is **paid panel research — never "reviews"** (UWG Anhang Nr. 23b/23c, § 5b Abs. 3; review L1): funder ToS prohibit presenting scores as organic consumer feedback, methodology page published.
5. **Unified identity-and-role matrix (review L2 meta-finding):** one document pinning each legal duty to one of three identity layers — (i) on-chain per-round one-time keys, (ii) stable pseudonymous rater ID at the receipt/DAC7 layer, (iii) vaulted real identity (retention-then-deletion design, § 147 AO) — and confirming with counsel that a pseudonymous receipt ID satisfies Art. 28 UStG disclosure. Launch scope: **B2B-only funders** (browser + x402, machine-readable trader self-identification in the 402 offer — review L4/B10); consumer checkout machinery deferred until a consumer funder exists.

## Part D — Risks and open decisions

1. **Sybil majorities in small rounds are now the top mechanism risk** (was: bond design). Peer-prediction scoring rewards a coordinated majority; the defenses are eligibility tiers, minimum revealed-voter floors scaled to bounty size, and verified-only high-value rounds (§A.4.4). Treat the tier/floor matrix as a first-class launch parameter and monitor with the correlation analytics from day one.
2. **Reputation-gaming replaces stake-gaming:** build-honest-then-spend attacks on probationary reputation are documented against Community Notes-style systems. Mitigations: negative-capable reputation, revocable attestations, correlation analytics feeding revocation. Accept as residual.
3. **EMT/PSD2 opinion could still come back adverse on the funder-side escrows** — unchanged from v1, now the only remaining fund-flow question.
4. **Immutability + bug = redeploy.** Accepted consciously; exit-path invariant, small surface, audit competition. Never ship a "temporary" pause.
5. **The issuer signer key is the one operational identity-trust point** (replaces the old config-freeze risk — off-chain verification means World upgrades and provider swaps never touch contracts). Compromise mints bogus eligibility but can never reach funds; mitigations: expiry + issuance caps + nonces (already in `WorldIdV4BackendIssuer`), monitoring issuance volume, and rotation by redeploying only the small issuer contract. Flip side to disclose honestly: verified-human eligibility is Hawig's attestation, not an independently verifiable on-chain proof — publish the verification policy and keep marketing provider-neutral.
6. **Sole-frontend intermediary optics** remain regardless of keys — mitigated by contract-first docs, IPFS mirror, honest "non-custodial, not decentralized-governance" language.
7. **Transparency logs only bite if someone recomputes** (the Sigstore caveat). Make recomputation one command, invite it in docs, and consider a small standing bounty for demonstrated mismatches — a real challenger incentive, priced honestly, without the on-chain theater.
8. **Agent-lane pricing is deferred, not solved:** if inference-cost spam materializes in open rounds before x402 submission pricing is legally cleared, the interim lever is eligibility tiers and per-identity caps on unverified/agent traffic.
9. **Coinbase concentration** (Base sequencer + Base Account + CDP Paymaster + possible CDP fallback wallets + x402 facilitator) is a real single-vendor exposure — accepted consciously because every piece rides open standards (4337/5792/7677/7702), so Pimlico/ZeroDev-class replacements are drop-in, and Coinbase's incentive is to subsidize Base apps. Revisit if CDP pricing or terms shift; never resolve it by taking key management in-house (that is licensable crypto custody in Germany and would end the non-custodial posture).
